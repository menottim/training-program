# Sleep, Recovery, and Fatigue Auto-Regulation

## Scope

Covers sleep's role in athletic adaptation, auto-regulation signals (RPE drift, HRV, subjective wellness), evidence rankings for common recovery modalities (cold water immersion, massage, foam rolling, stretching, sauna, active recovery, compression, contrast therapy), nutrition's role in recovery (protein distribution, alcohol effect), and overtraining red flags. Calibrated for a 40-year-old recreational athlete training ~6 hours/week (lifting + basketball + hockey) with bilateral Achilles tendinopathy. Does not cover clinical sleep disorders (OSA, chronic insomnia) or clinical fatigue pathology.

## Mechanism (what the controlled data actually shows)

### Sleep is the highest-ROI recovery intervention

During slow-wave sleep (N3), growth hormone pulses peak and the glymphatic system clears metabolic waste. REM sleep consolidates motor learning, which matters for skill-based sports. Chronic sleep restriction downregulates glucose tolerance, elevates cortisol, and shifts autonomic balance toward sympathetic dominance, compounding recovery debt.

Mah et al. (2011, Sleep 34(7):943-50) extended sleep to ~10 hours in bed for 5-7 weeks in 11 male D1 basketball players and found 282-foot sprint time improved by ~0.7s, free-throw and three-point accuracy increased by ~9% each, and subjective physical and mental well-being improved. The sample is small and specific, but the effect size is large. Knowles et al. (2018, J Sci Med Sport 21(9):959-68) systematically reviewed 17 studies on acute sleep deprivation and strength and found a consistent pattern: __compound multi-joint lifts (squat, deadlift, bench) degrade more than isolation work__, with motivation and central drive as the apparent mediators. Peripheral contractile function is largely preserved; what fails is the athlete's willingness or ability to recruit maximal effort.

Walsh et al. (2021, Br J Sports Med 55(7):356-68) IOC expert consensus explicitly argues against a one-size-fits-all athlete sleep prescription. Individual sleep need varies genetically, and may trend higher during heavy training blocks. The 7-9 hour range (Hirshkowitz et al. 2015 NSF consensus) is the floor for general health; athletes in active training blocks often need the upper end or more.

### Recovery modality evidence is clearer than most people think

Dupuy et al. (2018, Front Physiol 9:403) meta-analyzed head-to-head recovery interventions and found a clear hierarchy for reducing DOMS, fatigue, and biomarkers of muscle damage: __massage > active recovery ≈ compression garments ≈ cold water immersion > stretching (null)__. Guo et al. (2017, Front Physiol 8:747) specifically meta-analyzed massage and confirmed a large effect on DOMS. Wiewelhove et al. (2019, Front Physiol 10:376) meta-analyzed foam rolling and found small improvements in ROM and modest reductions in DOMS; performance effects are marginal.

Cold water immersion has a significant caveat. Leeder et al. (2012, Br J Sports Med 46(4):233-40) confirmed its acute soreness-reduction benefit, but Roberts et al. (2015, J Physiol 593(18):4285-301) demonstrated that chronic post-lifting CWI __blunts satellite cell activity and mTOR signaling__, reducing 12-week hypertrophy gains substantially. Practical implication: CWI is useful in-season when the priority is "perform tomorrow's game" but counterproductive during strength/hypertrophy blocks. Do not ice-bath routinely after lifting if growth is the goal.

Static stretching merits specific correction. Small et al. (2008, Res Sports Med 16(3):213-31) systematically reviewed pre-exercise static stretching for injury prevention and found __no protective effect__. Pre-exercise static stretching held >60 seconds can transiently reduce maximal force and power output by 3-5%. The Cochrane review by Herbert and de Noronha on stretching for DOMS found average ~1-point reduction on a 100-point scale, clinically meaningless.

### Overtraining is a continuum, not a state

Meeusen et al. (2013, MSSE 45(1):186-205 / Eur J Sport Sci 13(1):1-24) joint ECSS/ACSM consensus describes a continuum: __functional overreaching__ (short-term decrement followed by supercompensation, desirable) → __non-functional overreaching__ (weeks of stagnation, recovery possible with deload) → __overtraining syndrome__ (months of impairment, often requires complete rest and clinical intervention). No single biomarker reliably diagnoses OTS. The hallmarks are performance decrement + mood disturbance + sleep disruption + elevated resting HR + reduced HRV, usually combined.

Plews et al. (2013, Sports Med 43(9):773-81; 2014, Int J Sports Physiol Perform 9(5):783-90) reviewed HRV as an auto-regulation tool. Single-day readings are too noisy (sleep, alcohol, caffeine, stress, measurement variance). A 7-day rolling average with at least 3 readings/week is the minimum for useful trending. For recreational athletes, HRV is probably best used as a binary push/back-off heuristic rather than a prescription for exact training load.

### Alcohol has an outsized and specific cost

Parr et al. (2014, PLoS ONE 9(2):e88384) gave resistance-trained men post-exercise alcohol at 1.5 g/kg body weight (roughly 12 standard drinks for a 220-lb adult) with or without co-ingested protein, in a crossover design. Alcohol suppressed post-exercise myofibrillar protein synthesis by ~24% even with protein co-ingestion, and ~37% when carbs were the co-ingestion. The dose is high and unrealistic for typical social drinking, but the mechanism (mTOR signaling disruption) is dose-responsive. Even moderate intake (3-4 drinks) disrupts sleep architecture by suppressing REM in the second half of the night.

## Practical Application

1. __Target 7.5-9 hours in bed nightly with consistent bed and wake times (±30 min, including weekends).__ For M-F 9-5 schedule: lights-out 10:30-11 PM with 7 AM wake is a reasonable anchor. Walsh 2021 IOC consensus supports individualizing to perceived need. Consider a 20-minute nap (not >30 min, to avoid sleep inertia) on heavy training days if possible.

2. __Auto-regulate lifts using RPE and readiness heuristics, not gadget HRV.__ On days after basketball or hockey, if warm-up sets feel slow or first working set RPE is ≥1 grade higher than expected, drop top-set weight 10-15% or reduce volume. Knowles 2018 shows sleep-poor days hit compound lifts specifically; swap squat/deadlift variants for isolation work on those days if needed. Single-joint strength is largely preserved under sleep debt.

3. __Protein distribution beats timing precision__ (Aragon and Schoenfeld 2013). Target 1.6-2.2 g/kg/day (160-220 g for a 220-lb athlete) across 4 meals of ~0.4 g/kg each. Post-session protein within a few hours is sufficient; the 30-minute anabolic window is folklore. See `protein-distribution.md` for detailed dosing.

4. __Use CWI selectively, not routinely.__
   - __Yes__ after games when the next performance is within 48 hours and the priority is readiness over adaptation.
   - __No__ after lifting sessions during strength/hypertrophy blocks - Roberts 2015 shows chronic post-lifting CWI blunts muscle growth.
   - __Maybe__ contrast showers or cool-only baths (~12-15°C, 10 min) post-game, which are less extreme and less likely to suppress anabolic signaling.

5. __Recovery modality triage (highest to lowest ROI for time spent):__
   1. Sleep extension and consistency (free, highest evidence).
   2. Zone 2 active recovery walk or easy bike, 20-30 min on off days (aids fatigue clearance without added damage).
   3. Massage or percussion gun on sore muscle groups (largest DOMS effect size per Dupuy 2018).
   4. Foam rolling pre-session as warmup, post-session for ROM maintenance (small but real).
   5. Sauna 2-3x/week, 15-20 min, on non-training days (Scoon 2007 plasma volume benefits).
   6. Static stretching (fine for ROM goals but does not prevent injury, should not precede heavy lifting or explosive play).

6. __Alcohol has a real cost.__ Cap at 1-2 drinks on training days, not within 3 hours of bed. Parr 2014's 12-drink experiment is unrealistic but the mechanism is dose-responsive. Even 3-4 drinks disrupt REM in the second half of the night, compounding the next day's recruitment deficit.

7. __Overtraining red flags__ (any 3+ warrants a 7-10 day deload, not pushing through): performance plateau or decrement >2 weeks despite continued training; resting HR elevated >5 bpm above personal norm for 3+ consecutive days; persistent morning mood disturbance (irritability, lack of enthusiasm); unexplained weight loss; disrupted sleep despite adequate opportunity; frequent minor illnesses or slow-healing cuts; worsening Achilles pain at rest (not just during activity).

## Unresolved or Bleeding-Edge Questions

- __HRV-guided training for recreational athletes.__ Most research is on elite endurance athletes. The 7-day rolling-average heuristic (Plews 2014) is defensible but the exact load-adjustment thresholds for middle-aged mixed-sport athletes have not been validated.
- __Consumer wearable sleep-stage accuracy.__ Total sleep time detection is reasonable (~85-90% vs polysomnography), but REM and deep-stage classification is 60-79% at best (Chinoy 2021; Berryhill 2020). Treat the "deep sleep minutes" metric as noise; use TST and consistency.
- __Sauna as recovery vs adaptation stimulus.__ Scoon 2007 and Stanley 2015 suggest cardiovascular and plasma-volume benefits. Heat exposure activates HSPs and mTOR-independent pathways that may complement or interfere with training adaptations. Optimal placement (morning vs post-training vs rest day) is not established.
- __Peptide and supplement claims for tendon recovery__ (BPC-157, TB-500). No human RCTs; veterinary and rodent data are preliminary. Do not recommend.
- __Mouth taping, grounding/earthing, infrared mats.__ No quality evidence for recovery benefit.

- __2026-04-18:__ File initialized. Corrections to common gym-culture and prior CLAUDE.md claims:
  - __"30-60 minute anabolic window" is refuted.__ Aragon and Schoenfeld 2013 show the window is ~4-6 hours wide in fed athletes. Total daily protein and distribution matter far more than narrow post-workout timing.
  - __"Static stretching prevents injury" is refuted.__ Small 2008 and Herbert 2011 systematic reviews show no protective effect and potential transient force/power impairment at >60s holds.
  - __"Lactic acid causes DOMS" is refuted.__ Blood lactate clears within ~60 minutes post-exercise; DOMS peaks 24-72h later and is caused by mechanical microtrauma plus inflammatory response.
  - __"Ice baths after every workout help muscle growth" is refuted.__ Roberts 2015 and subsequent meta-analyses show chronic post-lifting CWI __suppresses__ hypertrophy. Use CWI for in-season game recovery, not routine lifting recovery during strength/hypertrophy blocks.
  - __"Stretching after exercise reduces soreness" is refuted.__ Cochrane review (Herbert and de Noronha 2011) found negligible effect (~1 point reduction on 100-point scale).
  - __"Everyone needs exactly 8 hours of sleep" is oversimplified.__ NSF 2015 range is 7-9 hours; Walsh 2021 IOC consensus recommends individualized targets. Athletes in heavy training may need the upper end.
  - __"HRV drops always mean overtraining" is oversimplified.__ Plews 2012 showed HRV can decrease during positive adaptation in some elite athletes. 7-day rolling trends combined with subjective wellness are more interpretable than single readings.
