# Blood Donation and Athletic Recovery (Power Red / Double Red Cell)

## Scope

Covers the physiological and performance consequences of whole blood and Double Red Cell (Power Red / DRCA) donation, the recovery timeline for oxygen-carrying capacity and endurance performance, and the role of iron supplementation in that recovery. Calibrated for Menotti, who is a regular Power Red donor (2-3x/year) and an in-season recreational basketball and hockey player. The iron-status and supplementation specifics are developed in [supplements.md](supplements.md); this file covers the performance side and the training response. Does not cover donation eligibility criteria or clinical management of anemia.

## Athlete context

Menotti donates Double Reds (Power Red), which removes __two units of red cells__ in one sitting (vs one unit for whole blood). Per [supplements.md](supplements.md): each Power Red removes ~470 mg iron (vs 220-250 mg for whole blood), full iron-store replacement takes 24-30 weeks, and the American Red Cross imposes a 112-day interdonation deferral for Power Red vs 56 days for whole blood. He already supplements 65 mg elemental iron + 125 mg vitamin C daily, which is appropriate and should be continued through the interdonation interval. The performance literature below is almost entirely __single-unit whole blood__; Power Red removes roughly double the red-cell mass, so magnitudes and timelines here are extrapolated upward and flagged accordingly.

## Key Findings

## Mechanism

### The acute hit runs on two separate clocks

Removing blood lowers performance through two distinct mechanisms that recover at different rates:

1. __Blood-volume loss (fast clock).__ Less circulating volume lowers stroke volume and cardiac filling, dropping VO2max immediately. Plasma volume re-expands within ~3 days through fluid shifts and retention, so this component largely resolves by day 3. Hill 2013 showed this clearly: plasma-only donation (which removes volume but not red cells) transiently cut time to exhaustion but did __not__ reduce VO2max once measured beyond the acute window, because the red cells were retained.

2. __Red-cell-mass deficit (slow clock).__ Fewer red cells means lower total hemoglobin and reduced oxygen-carrying capacity. This is the limiting factor and recovers only as fast as erythropoiesis can rebuild red cells, which is governed by erythropoietin, iron availability, and marrow capacity. For a single whole-blood unit this takes ~3-4 weeks (Meurrens 2016). For Power Red, with ~2 units removed, it is longer; the Red Cross 112-day deferral is the regulatory proxy for that extended red-cell-mass recovery.

The relationship between hemoglobin and aerobic capacity is roughly proportional: across the 11-16 g/dL range, each ~0.3 g/dL drop in hemoglobin is associated with roughly a 1% drop in VO2max. So the size of the aerobic hit tracks the size of the red-cell loss, which is why Power Red hits harder than whole blood.

### Why power and strength are spared while endurance suffers

The deficit is specifically in __oxygen delivery__. Maximal strength and short explosive efforts (jumps, sprints, a single hard shift) run primarily on the anaerobic ATP-PCr and glycolytic systems, which do not depend on oxygen transport in the moment. Endurance, repeated high-intensity bouts, and recovery between efforts depend on aerobic oxygen delivery and suffer most. Hill 2013 found anaerobic responses far less affected than aerobic ones. This is the basis for the practical observation an athlete reports as "wind is down but legs feel fine."

### Implication for intermittent court and ice sports

Basketball and hockey are intermittent: anaerobic-skill bursts separated by lower-intensity periods or bench time. The pure-endurance deficits measured on cycle-ergometer-to-exhaustion tests (the ~10-15% VO2max figures) overstate the effect on a 20-40 minute intermittent session. The real-world signal is elevated heart rate and RPE at a given effort, faster fatigue late in a session, and slower recovery between bouts/shifts, rather than a loss of skill or power.

## Iron: what it does and does not do

This is the most misunderstood part, and the nuance matters for a repeat donor.

- __Iron is the rate-limiting substrate for hemoglobin synthesis.__ Supplementation reliably and substantially speeds the return of hemoglobin and iron stores (Kiss 2015 / REDS-III: 23 weeks to 5 weeks in slow recoverers). This is Strong evidence and is the clinical basis for the Red Cross recommendation to supplement after a Power Red.
- __For a single donation, iron does not speed up performance recovery.__ Time-trial performance returns within ~1 week whether or not iron is taken (2025 JSCR), because in an iron-replete person the short-term performance rebound tracks plasma-volume restoration and early erythropoiesis, not iron availability.
- __For a repeat donor, iron does protect performance over time.__ Across repeated donations, supplementation limits the cumulative endurance decline (2020 RCT, PMID 32931412). Menotti's 2-3 Power Reds/year, each draining ~470 mg iron with 24-30 week store-replacement, is exactly the repeat-donor scenario where iron prevents a slow performance erosion that would otherwise accumulate. So his daily iron is doing real work, both hematologically and for performance continuity, even though it will not fast-forward the legs after any one donation.

Bottom line: iron is necessary maintenance for him, not an acute recovery accelerant. Do not expect it to shorten the wind deficit after a given Power Red.

## Practical Application (calibrated for Menotti)

1. __Continue daily iron, optimize absorption.__ Keep the 65 mg elemental iron + 125 mg vitamin C. Take it away from coffee, tea, and dairy/calcium, which inhibit absorption; the vitamin C pairing already roughly doubles non-heme uptake. Add dietary heme iron (red meat, poultry, fish), which absorbs far better than plant iron. (See [supplements.md](supplements.md) for the full dosing rationale and the peri-workout high-dose-antioxidant caution.)

2. __Train the deficit, do not fight it, for ~4-6 weeks post-Power-Red.__ Auto-regulate conditioning by RPE/feel, not pace or output; a given effort reads 1-2 RPE points high, which is the donation, not detraining. This window pairs well with a power-realization (Phase 3) block, because power work is the least oxygen-dependent training. Lengthen rest between power sets to keep each effort fresh (quality over density). Keep playing games (A-priority) and auto-regulate within them by shortening shifts/possessions and taking full recovery when wind dips.

3. __Support the rebuild.__ Hold the protein floor (160-220 g/day) since erythropoiesis needs protein for globin synthesis and adequate total calories. Stay well-hydrated to support plasma re-expansion in the first ~3 days, but do not overdrink (see [sodium-hydration.md](sodium-hydration.md): excess water dilutes serum sodium, it does not flush anything). Protect 7.5-9 h sleep (see [recovery-sleep.md](recovery-sleep.md)).

4. __Watch for donation-specific red flags__ in the first 24-72 h, which are distinct from normal training fatigue: lightheadedness, dizziness on hard stops, unusual breathlessness, or a heart rate that will not settle between efforts. These warrant reducing intensity/volume rather than pushing through.

5. __Monitor with ferritin.__ At the next donation or annual physical, request serum ferritin (target 30-200 ng/mL per [supplements.md](supplements.md)). Many Vitalant and Red Cross centers run ferritin at donation. If recovery feels like it is dragging well past the expected window, ferritin confirms whether iron status is the cause.

6. __Schedule donations around training intent.__ A Power Red lands most gently before a power/strength block (low aerobic dependence) and worst before a conditioning-heavy block or a peak game stretch. If a future donation is discretionary, time it into a low-aerobic-demand phase.

## Unresolved Questions

- __No direct Power Red (DRCA) performance-recovery study.__ All the performance timelines above are single-unit whole blood. The 4-6+ week DRCA estimate is extrapolated from the ~2x red-cell loss plus the 112-day regulatory deferral; the precise DRCA VO2max recovery curve is not established (Emerging).
- __Iron and performance recovery in repeat donors is supported by limited trials__ (one repeated-donation RCT, small single-donation trials). The effect direction is consistent but effect sizes and the dose/timing optimum for a 2-3x/year Power Red donor are not precisely characterized (Moderate).
- __Intermittent-sport-specific data is absent.__ The mapping from cycle-ergometer VO2max deficits to basketball/hockey performance is mechanistic reasoning, not measured.
