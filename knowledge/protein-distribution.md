# Protein: Daily Ceiling, Per-Meal Distribution, and Safety

## Scope

Covers optimal daily protein intake for resistance-trained athletes, per-meal anabolic windows, distribution strategies, and the safety of high-protein intake. Applies to decisions about meal planning, supplementation, and evaluating days with uneven protein distribution. Calibrated for a 40-year-old resistance-trained athlete; values are slightly higher than RDA-level recommendations.

## Mechanism (what the controlled data actually shows)

### Daily ceiling: ~1.6 g/kg for muscle gain, with a safety tail to 3.4+ g/kg

Morton et al. (2018) conducted a systematic review and meta-regression of 49 RCTs (1,863 participants) examining protein supplementation on resistance-training outcomes. They found a plateau in resistance-training-induced gains in fat-free mass at __1.62 g protein/kg/day__ (95% CI 1.03-2.20). Beyond this intake, additional protein did not further increase muscle mass or strength.

The confidence interval is wide (1.03 to 2.20), meaning individual requirements vary. The upper bound of 2.2 g/kg is a reasonable target for an individual who wants to maximize the upside without wasting intake. Increasing age reduces efficacy and resistance-training experience increases it, both moderators identified in the same meta-regression.

At intakes far above the muscle-building ceiling, protein is oxidized or converted to substrate via gluconeogenesis. It is not "wasted" in a nutritional sense (it contributes calories via the thermic effect of food at ~20-30% of protein calories, Westerterp 2004) but it does not contribute additional muscle mass.

### Per-meal ceiling: ~0.4 g/kg, not a fixed 20-25 g

The older "muscle full" hypothesis asserted that muscle protein synthesis (MPS) is maximally stimulated by ~20-25 g of high-quality protein per meal, with anything above being oxidized. Schoenfeld and Aragon (2018) reviewed this and concluded that the ceiling scales with body size rather than being absolute.

Their recommendation: __0.4 g/kg body weight per meal, across at least 4 meals, for a total of ~1.6 g/kg/day minimum__. For a 100-kg athlete, that means ~40 g per meal and ~160 g per day minimum. Higher per-meal doses can still contribute to anabolism, but with diminishing returns as the meal size exceeds the 0.4 g/kg mark.

Importantly, the 0.4 g/kg ceiling is based on fast-absorbed protein sources (e.g., whey). Slower-digesting protein (casein, whole-food mixed meals with fat and fiber) produces a longer absorption window and may raise the effective per-meal ceiling by extending the time MPS is elevated.

### Distribution matters: 4 meals beats 2 for the same total

Areta et al. (2013) directly tested three protein distribution patterns (8 × 10 g, 4 × 20 g, 2 × 40 g) in young men over 12 hours after resistance exercise, with the same 80 g total. The __4 × 20 g pattern produced significantly higher muscle protein synthesis__ than either the pulse (2 × 40 g) or bleed (8 × 10 g) patterns.

Mamerow et al. (2014) extended this to a 24-hour window in healthy adults and found an even distribution (~30 g/meal × 3 meals) produced 25% higher 24-hour MPS than a skewed distribution (~10 g breakfast, ~15 g lunch, ~65 g dinner) despite identical daily totals. Applied to a typical "big dinner" eating pattern, this is direct evidence that moving protein earlier in the day outperforms stacking it at night.

The magnitude of the effect is modest (10-25% of daily MPS), and the muscle-mass consequence over months is subtle rather than dramatic. It is worth optimizing if other variables (total intake, training, sleep) are already handled.

### Older adults have blunted per-meal MPS

Moore et al. (2015) demonstrated that older adults require a higher protein dose per meal to achieve the same MPS response as younger adults. The suggested per-meal dose for older adults was ~0.40 g/kg vs ~0.24 g/kg for younger adults. This "anabolic resistance" is partly reversible with resistance training (Breen and Phillips 2013) but suggests that 40-year-old and older athletes should target the upper end of the 0.4 g/kg per-meal range rather than the lower end.

### Safety: no evidence of harm up to 3.4 g/kg in healthy adults

Antonio et al. (2016) placed 14 resistance-trained men on a high-protein diet (~3.3-3.4 g/kg/day) for one year with no changes in kidney function, liver function, or blood lipids compared to their normal diet. A follow-up trial found the same dose improved body composition when combined with heavy resistance training, with no harmful markers.

A broader review (Antonio et al. 2020, Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition) confirms that intakes of 3.2-4.4 g/kg/day are well tolerated in healthy adults without preexisting kidney disease. The RDA of 0.8 g/kg is calibrated for minimum nitrogen balance in sedentary adults and underestimates requirements for training athletes.

Exception: individuals with chronic kidney disease or compromised renal function should consult a physician. The general safety conclusion applies to healthy adults only.

## Practical Application

1. __Daily target range: 1.6-2.2 g/kg body weight.__ For a 100-kg athlete, that is 160-220 g/day. Below 1.6 g/kg likely leaves some muscle-building potential on the table. Above 2.2 g/kg provides no additional muscle-building benefit but no harm either.

2. __Per-meal target: 0.4 g/kg × 4+ meals.__ For a 100-kg athlete, four meals of ~40 g each. Older athletes (40+) should aim closer to the top of this range per meal to overcome mild anabolic resistance.

3. __Timing matters less than total.__ The "anabolic window" immediately post-workout is largely a myth (Schoenfeld et al. 2013). Total daily intake distributed across meals is the primary variable. A pre-workout protein meal taken within a few hours before training covers the post-workout window adequately.

4. __Distribution beats concentration for the same total.__ If your current pattern is 0 g breakfast, 30 g lunch, 90 g dinner, moving toward 30 g × 4 will likely produce more muscle protein synthesis from the same daily total. Do not, however, sacrifice quality meals to chase perfect distribution.

5. __Excess protein is calories first, muscle second.__ A day with 250-300 g protein intake is not harmful, but the muscle-building benefit plateaus around 1.6-2.2 g/kg. The extra 50-100 g is oxidized for energy (though with a higher thermic cost than carbs or fat, about 20-30% vs 5-10% for carbs and 0-3% for fat, per Westerterp 2004).

6. __Supplements are a tool, not a requirement.__ Whole-food protein is equally effective. Whey, casein, and mixed plant sources are convenient and cost-effective ways to hit targets. ISSN position stand (Jäger et al. 2017) notes that neither source nor timing meaningfully shifts long-term outcomes when total intake is adequate.

## Unresolved or Bleeding-Edge Questions

- __Optimal per-meal dose in older trained adults.__ The ~0.4 g/kg number comes primarily from young-adult studies. Moore et al. 2015 and Traylor et al. 2018 suggest older adults benefit from higher per-meal doses (~0.6 g/kg), but the data is less robust.
- __Plant vs animal protein for muscle-building at equivalent doses.__ Emerging evidence suggests blended plant sources approach animal-source effectiveness when total intake is adequate, but the per-meal equivalence is not fully established.
- __Leucine threshold as a practical gate.__ The idea that each meal must contain ~2.5-3 g leucine to trigger MPS is mechanistically supported but not clearly operationalized in whole-food meal planning outside supplement design.
- __Protein's role in tendon remodeling.__ Direct evidence for protein (particularly collagen + vitamin C) supporting tendinopathy recovery is emerging but not yet consensus (Shaw et al. 2017; Clifford et al. 2019). Flag as Emerging when discussing HSR-adjacent nutrition.
